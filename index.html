Helen Jackson

Doctor, technologist, researcher

Things I like: people, open, good science, common sense.

About me

I am currently a freelance health technologist and researcher, mainly working in academia and community/not-for-profit organisations.

My goal is to produce, promote and de-mystify good technology and good science, in order to support increasingly useful collaboration between an increasingly wide variety of experts, ultimately for the benefit of patient health and care.

I graduated in medicine from King's College London in 2017 (the graduate-entry program). Before medicine, I completed a PhD in auditory signal processing and a BA in Natural Sciences, both at the University of Cambridge.

Things I do and think

I work on end-user-facing programs and products, in web development, and also in academic writing, data science, experimental methods, and statistics.

I enjoy general technical project management and strategy design whether I get my hands dirty or not.

I like identifying real-world problems based on user-need, and prototyping quick, light-handed and sustainable solutions to them, rather than taking something that just happens to have been made already and searching for problems to solve with it.

I believe in the value of domain experts in multi-disciplinary projects. I recognise that asking advice early, ensuring understanding of how it applies to the task in hand, and then trusting it, very often saves hassle, face and time.

I am passionate about designing technical infrastructure to allow sensible working processes between collaborators with differing levels of technical skills ...and interest.

I enjoy writing for a range of audiences. I particularly enjoy writing technical documentation for different audiences, which in my opinion is an activity overlooked with cost.

I am passionate about open access academic publishing. I believe that proponents of the old-fashioned subscription models of publishing should consider carefully the value they can now add in this fast, digital age, and shift their focus accordingly to address real user needs, rather than simply propping up outdated business models. I believe that wide dissemination of academic research will engage new audiences, and that transparency will foster rigourous debate, reducing the chance that future research might be built on errors.

I am similarly passionate about promoting open source software in order to support full interoperability between the numerous components in the NHS's technical infrastructure. For too long, our ability to collect and use data from patients' health records has been dictated by a small number of for-profit commercial companies.
